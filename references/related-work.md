# Background and Prior Evidence — Writing Guide (Medical Research)

Place the present study against the most relevant prior evidence so that the novelty and the rationale for the present design are easy to verify.

In medical papers, "related work" appears in three forms:

1. **Inside the Introduction** of an original article, as one or two paragraphs framing the prior evidence and the gap.
2. **As the body** of a narrative review, where mapping and synthesizing the literature is the paper's central contribution.
3. **As a preliminary section** in a systematic review or meta-analysis, before the formal PRISMA-aligned methods. The systematic synthesis itself follows PRISMA (see `references/reporting-standards.md`).

This guide covers all three. The structural principles draw on Denney & Tewksbury (2013) "How to Write a Literature Review" and on standard biomedical practice.

## Workflow

1. Search systematically (PubMed/MEDLINE + Embase + Cochrane CENTRAL + appropriate registries; LILACS / SciELO when relevant) so the prior evidence list is comprehensive, not only the studies you remember.
2. List the directly competing trials, the most relevant cohort studies, and the most recent systematic reviews.
3. Group the literature by clinical theme (population × intervention/exposure × outcome × design), not by year.
4. For each theme, summarize the common design pattern, then the key methodological limitation relevant to your gap.
5. End each theme with one sentence stating your study's distinction.
6. End the section as a "closing argument": explicitly state the gap that the present study fills.

## The Funnel — Broad to Narrow

A good literature framing is shaped like a funnel: it opens wide on the disease or clinical question, narrows through the most relevant prior evidence, and ends at the precise gap your study addresses.

```
Broad: clinical importance / disease burden
  -> Established consensus or guideline-level statement
  -> Most relevant prior trials / cohorts / reviews
    -> Limitation of those studies (population, design, follow-up, outcome)
      -> The remaining unresolved question
        -> What this study contributes
Narrow: aim and hypothesis of the present paper
```

If the section reads from broad to narrow without lateral excursions, the rationale for the study becomes self-evident.

## Three Common Foci (Cooper, 1984)

A literature framing usually takes one of three foci. Choose deliberately.

1. **Integrative.** Summarize what prior research collectively concludes about the question. Most empirical original articles need this.
2. **Theoretical or mechanistic.** Compare competing biological or clinical mechanisms that could explain the phenomenon. Common in mechanistic studies, biomarker studies, and target trial emulations.
3. **Methodological.** Highlight the methodological approaches used in prior research and their contributions. Common when the present study introduces a new design or analytic step (target trial emulation, individual-participant-data meta-analysis, network meta-analysis, Bayesian re-analysis).

If your study contributes a new method, your background section should foreground methodological foci, not findings.

## Topic Design

Use 2 to 4 focused themes. Typical patterns:

1. Trials of the standard-of-care intervention.
2. Trials or cohorts of the comparator intervention.
3. Mechanistic / biomarker evidence supporting the hypothesis.
4. Auxiliary methodology your study builds on (target trial emulation, IPD meta-analysis, Mendelian randomization).

## Paragraph Template — Theme by Theme

1. **Topic sentence.** Define the scope of this theme and its relevance to the present question.
2. **Representative evidence.** Summarize the key trial(s), cohort(s), or review in one to two sentences each. State design, population, primary outcome, and effect estimate. Include classic studies and the most recent advance.
3. **Limitation tied to the present gap.** What does this evidence not address that the present study does?
4. **Transition.** Connect smoothly to the next theme — these connecting sentences are the "welds" between themes; if they are weak, the section feels like an annotated bibliography.

## Anti-Pattern — The Annotated Bibliography

The most common literature-review failure is a string of paragraphs of the form:

```
Smith et al. (2019) found X.
Jones et al. (2020) found Y.
Lee et al. (2022) found Z.
```

This reads as a list, not a synthesis. Reviewers and readers learn nothing about what the field collectively knows or where it disagrees. Replace it with theme-organized prose:

```
Three lines of evidence support [HYPOTHESIS]. First, ...
Second, ...
Third, ...
However, all three lines share the limitation that ...
```

## Sentence Skeletons

1. `[Topic] has been studied in [N] [trials / cohorts / reviews] over the past [years].`
2. `In [Author Year], a [design] of [N] participants reported [effect estimate; 95% CI].`
3. `Across these studies, [pattern]; however, [shared limitation].`
4. `Whether [SPECIFIC QUESTION] in [POPULATION / SETTING] holds when tested with [DESIGN] therefore remains unanswered.`
5. `In this study, we address this gap by [design choice].`

## Outlining — A Living Document

Outline the section before drafting prose, but expect the outline to change. Many authors describe two writing phases:

1. **Writing to Understand.** Early drafts in which you, the writer, work out what the literature actually says. The structure can be ugly; the goal is clarity for yourself.
2. **Writing to Be Understood.** Revised drafts in which you reorganize for the reader. Each theme is now a clean paragraph; transitions are smooth; the funnel narrows from broad to specific.

If the outline does not change at all during writing, the section is probably weak — you missed something during drafting.

## Sources — What Counts

In order of evidentiary weight for medical research:

1. **Peer-reviewed empirical articles** — RCTs, prospective cohorts, systematic reviews and meta-analyses with low risk of bias.
2. **Authoritative guidelines** — WHO, NICE, USPSTF, specialty societies (e.g., ESC, ADA, ACC/AHA).
3. **Pre-registered protocols and statistical analysis plans** — particularly for citing prespecified intent.
4. **Authoritative books** — e.g., Hernán and Robins's *Causal Inference: What If* for methodological backing.
5. **Government and intergovernmental publications** — CDC, FDA, EMA, WHO publications for surveillance and policy.
6. **Conference abstracts and preprints** — cite with explicit caution, mark as preprint with DOI; reviewers expect peer-reviewed evidence wherever possible.
7. **Newspapers and magazines** — almost never appropriate; only for narrowly defined contextual claims (e.g., timing of a public health emergency).

Avoid:

1. **Predatory journals.** Verify journal credibility through DOAJ membership, Cabells, or PubMed indexing.
2. **Textbooks.** They cite older primary sources that you should cite directly.
3. **Reviews when a primary source is available.** Cite the primary trial or cohort, not a review of it.

## Classic Plus Recent

Include both:

1. The cornerstone study or studies that the field rests on.
2. The most recent trial, cohort, or systematic review on the question.

The classic citations show that the authors are well versed in the field's foundations. The recent citations show that the present paper engages the contemporary evidence and is not duplicating recently completed work.

## Citation Order Rules

In Vancouver-style numeric citation systems (used by most medical journals), citations are numbered in the order they first appear in the text. After every revision pass:

1. Scan the manuscript top-to-bottom and verify that citation numbers appear in ascending order.
2. After moving any sentence, re-check whether the affected citations need renumbering.
3. The reference list must match the in-text numbering exactly.

For author-date styles (Annals, some specialty journals), list references alphabetically by first author surname.

## Closing the Section — The "Closing Argument"

The end of the background section is the bridge to the present study. It is the literature-review equivalent of a prosecutor's closing argument: a concise, calibrated summary of what the prior evidence collectively shows, what it leaves unresolved, and why the present study is needed.

Sentence skeleton:

1. `Across [N] [trials / cohorts / reviews], [established conclusion].`
2. `However, [SPECIFIC GAP] is unresolved because [METHODOLOGICAL REASON].`
3. `Whether [PRECISE QUESTION] in [POPULATION] when tested with [DESIGN] is unknown.`
4. `In this study, we [aim] to address this gap.`

Avoid trailing off into "more research is needed". State exactly what kind of study answers the question.

## Narrative Review-Specific Notes

When the paper is a narrative review (not a systematic review), the entire body is the literature framing. Additional rules:

1. Make the search reproducible: state databases, dates, terms.
2. Make the inclusion/exclusion criteria explicit, even if narrative reviews allow more discretion than systematic reviews.
3. Use a clear thematic structure with headings.
4. Conclude each subsection with a synthesis sentence, not a transitional cliché.
5. Acknowledge the inherent selectivity of a narrative review as a limitation.

## Systematic Review-Specific Notes

When the paper is a systematic review or meta-analysis, the formal section to consult is `references/method.md` and `references/reporting-standards.md`. Briefly:

1. Follow the PRISMA 2020 checklist.
2. Register the protocol prospectively in PROSPERO (or equivalent).
3. Report the search strategy in full in the supplement.
4. Use a recognized risk-of-bias tool (RoB 2, ROBINS-I, QUADAS-2, Newcastle-Ottawa).
5. Use GRADE for certainty of evidence ratings on each outcome.
6. State prespecified subgroup and sensitivity analyses; mark post-hoc analyses as exploratory.
7. Provide a PRISMA flow diagram.

## Do and Don't

1. **Do** compare design, population, follow-up, outcome ascertainment, and analysis — not just headline results.
2. **Do** acknowledge the strongest competing evidence; do not hide a recent trial that contradicts your hypothesis.
3. **Do** explain why a contradicting study has a different result (population, dose, duration, era).
4. **Do** mix classic and recent citations.
5. **Do not** make Background a citation dump. Each citation should support a specific claim.
6. **Do not** invent citations. Every reference must be a real, verifiable publication. If a fact lacks a known source, mark it `[CITATION NEEDED]` and stop until the author resolves it.
7. **Do not** cite review articles when you should cite the primary source.
8. **Do not** double-cite (citing both a primary trial and a review of it for the same claim) without justification.
9. **Do not** write a paragraph-per-study annotated bibliography.

## Background Section Quality Checklist

1. Is the section funnel-shaped (broad → narrow)?
2. Are the strongest and most recent prior studies covered?
3. Is each theme clearly tied to the present clinical question?
4. Is the gap stated in methodological / clinical terms (population, design, outcome) rather than rhetorical terms ("no study has done X")?
5. Are citations real and traceable (PubMed ID, DOI, registry number)?
6. Are citations in order of first appearance?
7. Does the section close with an explicit "closing argument" stating the gap and bridging to the present study?
8. Is the section free of em-dashes in body sentences? (Use commas, semicolons, or parentheses.)
9. For systematic reviews: are PRISMA, registration, and risk-of-bias steps documented?
