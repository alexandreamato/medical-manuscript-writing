# Citation Styles — Detail (Non-Vancouver)

## AMA (JAMA Network)

AMA is similar to Vancouver but with notable differences:

1. In-text: superscript numerals (`¹`).
2. Author list: up to 6 authors before "et al." (some AMA journals allow all authors regardless of number — check).
3. Title case for article titles in older AMA editions; sentence case in 11th edition (modernized).
4. Journal abbreviation per NLM.
5. DOI included as `doi:10.xxxx/xxxxx`.

Example:

```
1. Solomon SD, McMurray JJV, Anand IS, et al. Angiotensin-neprilysin
   inhibition in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction. N Engl J
   Med. 2019;381(17):1609-1620. doi:10.1056/NEJMoa1908655
```

AMA-specific details that differ from Vancouver:

- **Et al. threshold.** AMA 10 used the "first three then et al." rule for more than three authors in the reference list; AMA 11 uses "first six then et al." Verify the edition required by the journal.
- **Title case (older AMA).** Older AMA editions used title case for article titles. AMA 11 moved to sentence case to align with Vancouver.
- **Punctuation around the DOI.** AMA places a period after the DOI; some Vancouver variants omit it. Follow the journal.

When the journal says "AMA style" without specifying an edition, default to AMA 11.

## APA (7th ed.)

APA is **author-date**, used by some health-sciences, behavioural, public-health, nursing, and medical-education journals.

In-text:

- Single author: `(Smith, 2020)` or `Smith (2020) reported ...`.
- Two authors: `(Smith & Jones, 2020)` or `Smith and Jones (2020) reported ...`.
- Three or more authors: `(Solomon et al., 2019)` from the first citation onward.

Reference list (alphabetical by first author surname):

```
Solomon, S. D., McMurray, J. J. V., Anand, I. S., Ge, J., Lam, C. S. P.,
   Maggioni, A. P., ... Pfeffer, M. A. (2019). Angiotensin-neprilysin
   inhibition in heart failure with preserved ejection fraction. New
   England Journal of Medicine, 381(17), 1609-1620.
   https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1908655
```

Rules:

1. Use up to 20 authors before truncating with "..." plus the final author (APA 7).
2. Year in parentheses immediately after the author list.
3. Sentence case for article title; title case for journal name.
4. Volume in italics (in print); issue in parentheses, not italicized.
5. Full DOI as a URL: `https://doi.org/...`.

Quotation conventions:

- Direct quote with page number: `(Smith, 2020, p. 14)` or `(Smith, 2020, pp. 14-15)`.
- For unpaginated electronic sources, use a paragraph number or section heading: `(Smith, 2020, para. 5)`.

Common APA-only items in medical writing:

- **Personal communications** (e.g., interview, email): cited in text only, not in the reference list: `(J. Smith, personal communication, May 1, 2024)`.
- **Datasets** require a version number and a DOI in the reference list.

## Harvard

Harvard is a **generic name** for many author-date styles. Each publisher has a slightly different version. Always check the Instructions to Authors. Common features:

1. In-text: `(Smith 2020)` or `(Smith and Jones 2020)` or `(Smith et al. 2020)` for ≥3 authors.
2. Reference list alphabetical by first author surname.
3. Year follows the author block, sometimes in parentheses.

Because "Harvard" varies, the only reliable rule is to follow the journal's worked example exactly. If the journal provides an Instruction PDF, copy three reference types (journal article, book, online resource) from that PDF into a local template before drafting.

## Chicago / Turabian (18th edition)

Two flavors:

1. **Notes-Bibliography.** Footnotes or endnotes for citations; full bibliography at the end. Used in humanities-leaning health journals.
2. **Author-Date.** In-text `(Smith 2020)` plus alphabetical reference list. More common in scientific writing and the variant most likely to appear in medical contexts.

The Chicago 18th edition (2024) is the current version of *The Chicago Manual of Style*.

### Chicago 18th Author-Date — In-Text Format

Two presentation modes:

| Mode | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Information prominent (citation in parentheses) | `It could be argued that mental flexibility is a key factor in well-being (Palladino and Wade 2010, 4).` |
| Author prominent (author named in sentence, year in parentheses) | `Palladino and Wade (2010, 4) argue that mental well-being is linked with flexible thinking.` |

Number-of-authors rules:

| Authors | In-text format |
| --- | --- |
| 1 author | `(Smith 2020)` |
| 2 authors | `(Smith and Jones 2020, 6)` |
| 3 or more authors | `(Thomas et al. 2018, 23)` (always use et al. from the third author onward, in every citation) |
| Organisation | `(World Health Organization 2018, para. 5)` |
| Organisation with abbreviation | First citation: `(World Health Organization [WHO] 2018)`; subsequent: `(WHO 2018)` |
| No date available | `(NASA, n.d.)` |

Disambiguation rules:

1. **Multiple sources in one citation** are separated by semicolons inside the same brackets, ordered alphabetically, chronologically, or by importance: `(Hegerl 1996, 12; Levitus et al. 2017, 4; Santer et al. 2003, 42)`.
2. **Multiple works by the same author(s)** in one citation: list years chronologically: `(Smith et al. 2019, 2023, 2025)`.
3. **Same author(s), same year**: append a, b, c (ordered alphabetically by title in the reference list): `(Clarke and Fawcett 2014a)`, `(Clarke and Fawcett 2014b)`.
4. **Different first authors with the same surname**: include initials in-text: `(J. Smith 2020)` vs. `(R. Smith 2020)`.
5. **Quoting from a secondary source**: cite as `(Original Author Year, page, quoted in Citing Author Year, page)`.

Quotations:

1. Short quotations (under ~5 lines) appear inline with quotation marks; the page number follows the year: `(Smith 2020, 14)`.
2. Long quotations (block quotations) are indented without quotation marks; the citation goes after the closing punctuation.

### Chicago 18th Author-Date — Reference List Format

General rules:

1. Heading: `References`, centred at the top of a new page.
2. Order: alphabetical by first author surname (or by organisation name; for works with no author, by first significant word of the title, ignoring `A`, `An`, `The`).
3. Hanging indent of ~1.27 cm (0.5 inch) on each entry.
4. Multiple works by the same author(s) in **different** years: order chronologically (oldest first); `n.d.` entries appear last.
5. Multiple works by the same author(s) in the **same** year: order alphabetically by title; append a, b, c to the year.
6. Single-author entry precedes multi-author entries beginning with the same name (`Gao 2011` before `Gao and McCarthy 2015`).
7. **Title case** for all titles (capitalize each significant word).
8. Article titles: enclosed in straight double quotation marks. Journal/book titles: italicised.
9. **DOIs** are preceded by `https://doi.org/` (presented as plain text with a trailing full stop). DOI is preferred over URL when both exist. If no DOI is available, include the URL or the database name.

#### Journal article

Format:

```
Author Surname, First Name(s). Year. "Article Title." Journal Title in Italics Volume (Issue): Pages. https://doi.org/DOI...
```

Example (medical):

```
Naghavi, Mohsen, Amanuel Alemu Abajobir, Cristiana Abbafati, et al. 2017.
"Global, Regional, and National Age-Sex Specific Mortality for 264 Causes
of Death, 1980-2016: A Systematic Analysis for the Global Burden of
Disease Study 2016." The Lancet 390 (10100): 1151-1210.
https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(17)32152-9.
```

Notes:

1. **First author**: Surname, First Name(s). Subsequent authors: First Name(s) Surname.
2. List up to **10 authors** in the reference list (per Chicago 18); for 11 or more, list the first 7 followed by `et al.`. Some journals impose a stricter limit (e.g., first 3 + et al.); check the Instructions to Authors.
3. **Article number / eLocator** (e.g., for *PLOS ONE*): use the article number in place of the page range: `PLOS ONE 14 (11): e0225770`.
4. **No volume number**: a comma precedes the issue number, with a month or season in brackets if available: `25 (May): 56-59`.
5. **No issue number**: list the page numbers after the volume separated by a colon with no space: `25:56-59`.
6. **Supplement**: indicate as `Suppl.1` or `(2, Suppl. 5)`.
7. **Advanced online publication**: use `ahead of print` plus the posted date: `2023. "Title." Biological Bulletin, ahead of print, April 5. https://doi.org/...`.
8. **Forthcoming** (accepted but not yet published): use `Forthcoming` in place of the year — `Smith, Margaret. Forthcoming. ...`. In-text: `(Smith, forthcoming)`.

#### Book

Format:

```
Author Surname, First Name(s). Year. Book Title in Italics. # ed. Publisher. https://doi.org/DOI... or URL.
```

Example:

```
Hernán, Miguel A., and James M. Robins. 2020. Causal Inference: What If.
Chapman & Hall/CRC.
```

#### Chapter in an edited book

Format:

```
Chapter Author Surname, First Name(s). Year. "Title of Chapter." In Title of Book in Italics, # ed., edited by Editor First Name Last Name. Publisher. https://doi.org/DOI...
```

Example:

```
Tierney, William G., and Michael Lanford. 2016. "Conceptualizing Innovation
in Higher Education." In Higher Education: Handbook of Theory and
Research, edited by Michael B. Paulsen. Springer.
https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-319-26829-3.
```

#### Webpage / online document

Format:

```
Author Surname, First Name(s) [or Organisation]. Year. "Title of Page." Site Name. URL.
```

If the page has no publication year, use `n.d.` and add an access date: `Accessed January 31, 2024. URL`.

#### Government or organisation report

```
Department of Education and Training. 2024. "During Your Studies in Australia."
Australian Government. https://www.education.gov.au/...
```

#### Dataset

```
Author Surname, First Name(s) [or Organisation]. Year. Dataset Title. Repository.
Version. https://doi.org/DOI...
```

#### Conference paper or poster

```
Chadwick, M. B., M. Blann, and S. Hilaire. 2008. "Multistep Direct
Preequilibrium Reactions and the Monte Carlo Approach," paper presented
at the International Conference on Nuclear Data for Science and
Technology, Paris, France, April 23-27, 2007.
https://doi.org/10.1051/ndata:07659.
```

#### Thesis or dissertation

```
Author Surname, First Name(s). Year. "Title of Thesis." [PhD diss. / Master's thesis], Institution. URL.
```

#### Generative AI

Chicago 18 explicitly addresses generative AI. Cite the tool when its output is used:

```
OpenAI. 2024. ChatGPT (May 13 version) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com.
```

Many medical journals **prohibit** AI as an author and require disclosure of AI use in the Methods or Acknowledgments. See `references/ethics-and-integrity.md`.

### Chicago 18th — Notes-Bibliography Variant

For the footnote-based variant:

1. First citation in a footnote includes the full reference: `1. Author First Name Last Name, *Book Title* (Publisher, Year), Page.`
2. Subsequent citations in footnotes use a short form: `2. Author Last Name, *Short Title*, Page.`
3. The bibliography at the end mirrors the Author-Date reference list but uses different punctuation (commas instead of periods around the year, etc.).

Most medical journals that use Chicago use the **Author-Date** variant. Verify in the Instructions to Authors which variant is required.

## CSE (Council of Science Editors)

Three options:

1. **Citation-Sequence** (numeric). In-text numbers in order of appearance; reference list in numeric order. Most similar to Vancouver.
2. **Citation-Name** (numeric, alphabetical reference list). In-text numbers refer to entries in an alphabetised reference list; the same source keeps its number throughout the manuscript.
3. **Name-Year** (author-date). In-text `(Smith 2020)`; reference list alphabetical by first author surname.

Specify which CSE variant the journal requires before formatting.

CSE-specific conventions:

- Journal title abbreviations follow the NLM list (same as Vancouver/AMA).
- Article titles are in sentence case.
- Authors are listed as Surname Initials (e.g., `Smith JA`), like Vancouver.
- DOIs are required when available and presented as `doi:10.xxxx/xxxxx` (no `https://doi.org/` prefix in classical CSE).

## Cross-style notes

Three patterns recur across all styles. Reviewers commonly flag them.

1. **Mixed styles in one manuscript.** Switching reference-manager styles mid-draft can leave a handful of legacy entries in the wrong style. Always reformat the entire reference list after switching styles, then spot-check 5 to 10 entries.
2. **Inconsistent author-list truncation.** Either always list up to N authors then `et al.`, or always list all. Mixing the two within one manuscript is a frequent reviewer complaint.
3. **Quotation marks vs. italics.** Vancouver/AMA italicize journal names and use no quotation marks for article titles; APA italicizes journal names without quotation marks; Chicago Author-Date uses quotation marks around article titles plus italics for journal names. Mismatch is usually a sign the wrong CSL style is loaded.
