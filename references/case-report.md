# Case Report Writing Guide (Medical Research)

This guide combines the practical worksheet of McCarthy & Reilly (Fam Med 2000;32(3):190-5) with the CARE 2013 reporting checklist. Case reports remain a valid form of scientific publication when they describe something genuinely new; they are also one of the easiest ways to make a methodological mistake (over-claiming, redundant literature review, identifiable patient information).

## When a Case Report Is Worth Writing

A case is suitable for a case report only when it is genuinely unique. Variation from a previously reported case is not enough on its own. A case meets the bar when it has at least one of these characteristics:

1. It contributes to a change in the course of medical science (e.g., the first published account of AIDS in 1981 was a case report).
2. It illustrates a new principle, supports or refutes a current theory, or stimulates research.
3. It presents a therapeutic or diagnostic observation that elucidates a previously misunderstood condition or response.
4. It demonstrates an adverse drug reaction or presumed cause-and-effect not previously detected or reported.
5. It shows an unusual combination of conditions, cascading events, or presenting complaints that confused decision-making or created treatment dilemmas.
6. It documents the impact of one disease or treatment on another, with an unexpected outcome.
7. It describes the personal influence the case had on the patient, the physician, or both — provided the lesson generalizes.

A new or unusual location for a previously recognized disease does not by itself justify a case report unless it comes with previously undocumented symptoms or required a particularly long, costly diagnostic process.

## Watchwords

**Brevity and clarity.** Most published case reports are under three journal pages, and the majority are one page or less. Reviewers and editors penalize bloat. Include only the essential information. Use tables, flow charts, and figures to replace prose where possible. Do not include "red herrings" (incidental abnormal findings unrelated to the case) unless they are likely to mislead other physicians, in which case explain why.

## Structure (Five Sections + Title and References)

This is the McCarthy & Reilly worksheet, updated for current journal practice. Most journals require all five sections, plus a structured CARE-style abstract and a patient consent statement.

```
Title
Authors and affiliations

1) Abstract (CARE-structured: Background; Case presentation; Conclusions)
2) Case history / report
   A. Description of patient (age, sex, ethnicity, occupation, relevant background)
   B. History of presenting condition
   C. Physical exam
   D. Relevant laboratory, imaging, and other tests
   E. Initial diagnosis and treatment
   F. Expected outcome
   G. Actual outcome (timeline)
3) Literature search (the methods of the case report)
   A. Databases searched
   B. Search terms
   C. Results (number relevant; key findings)
4) Discussion
   A. Relevant literature in context
   B. Hypothesis or proposed mechanism
   C. Diagnostic process / course of illness (table or algorithm preferred)
   D. Outcomes (drug-drug interactions, drug-condition interactions,
      conflicting outcomes / observations)
5) Conclusions / recommendations (the lesson learned)
6) Patient perspective (CARE recommends; required by some journals)
7) Informed consent statement
References
```

## CARE 2013 Checklist — Full Item List

The official CARE 2013 checklist (https://www.care-statement.org/) has 13 numbered items grouped into 11 topics. Use the table below as the master checklist for the manuscript and submit it as a supplementary file with page or line numbers indicating where each item is addressed (see `references/reporting-standards.md` for the checklist-completion template).

| Item | Topic | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Title | The diagnosis or intervention of primary focus, followed by the words "case report". |
| 2 | Key words | 2 to 5 key words identifying diagnoses or interventions, including "case report". |
| 3a | Abstract — Introduction | What is unique about this case and what does it add to the scientific literature? |
| 3b | Abstract — Symptoms | Main symptoms and important clinical findings. |
| 3c | Abstract — Diagnosis / intervention / outcomes | Main diagnoses, therapeutic interventions, and outcomes. |
| 3d | Abstract — Conclusion | Main "take-away" lesson(s). |
| 4 | Introduction | One or two paragraphs summarizing why this case is unique. May include references. |
| 5a | Patient information — demographics | De-identified patient-specific information. |
| 5b | Patient information — primary concerns | Primary concerns and symptoms of the patient. |
| 5c | Patient information — history | Medical, family, and psycho-social history including relevant genetic information. |
| 5d | Patient information — past interventions | Relevant past interventions **with outcomes**. |
| 6 | Clinical findings | Physical examination and significant clinical findings. |
| 7 | Timeline | Historical and current information from this episode of care organized as a **timeline**. Build at https://enciclopedia.med.br/care-timeline (preferred; supports section / hybrid / date modes) or use the Mermaid templates in `references/diagrams.md`. |
| 8a | Diagnostic assessment — testing | Diagnostic methods (PE, laboratory testing, imaging, surveys). |
| 8b | Diagnostic assessment — challenges | Diagnostic challenges (access to testing, financial, cultural). |
| 8c | Diagnostic assessment — diagnosis | Diagnosis, including other diagnoses considered. |
| 8d | Diagnostic assessment — prognosis | Prognosis (e.g., staging in oncology) where applicable. |
| 9a | Therapeutic intervention — type | Types (pharmacologic, surgical, preventive, self-care). |
| 9b | Therapeutic intervention — administration | Administration details (dosage, strength, duration). |
| 9c | Therapeutic intervention — changes | Changes in the therapeutic intervention with rationale. |
| 10a | Follow-up and outcomes — assessment | Clinician-assessed and patient-assessed outcomes (if available). |
| 10b | Follow-up and outcomes — testing | Important follow-up diagnostic and other test results. |
| 10c | Follow-up and outcomes — adherence | Intervention adherence and tolerability — and how this was assessed. |
| 10d | Follow-up and outcomes — adverse events | Adverse and unanticipated events. |
| 11a | Discussion — strengths and limitations | Scientific discussion of **both** strengths AND limitations of this case report. |
| 11b | Discussion — literature | Discussion of the relevant medical literature with references. |
| 11c | Discussion — scientific rationale | Scientific rationale for any conclusions, including assessment of possible causes. |
| 11d | Discussion — take-away | Primary "take-away" lessons in a **one-paragraph conclusion without references**. |
| 12 | Patient perspective | The patient shares their perspective in **one to two paragraphs** on the treatment(s) received. |
| 13 | Informed consent | Did the patient give informed consent? **Yes / No.** Provide the form on request. |

Items most often missed by reviewers and editors: **Item 7 (timeline)**, **Item 8d (prognosis when applicable)**, **Item 11d (take-away in a one-paragraph conclusion without references)**, and **Item 12 (patient perspective in one to two paragraphs)**.

## Section-by-Section Guidance

### Title

1. Descriptive, not a question. Includes the diagnosis or the unique feature.
2. Always contains the phrase "a case report" (or "case series" for 2 to 10 cases).
3. Avoid puns or marketing language.

Examples:

1. `Toothpaste allergy presenting as intractable perioral rash in a 10-year-old boy: a case report.`
2. `Resolution of chronic hepatitis B after ritonavir treatment in an HIV-infected patient.`

### Abstract (under 150 to 200 words; CARE-structured)

Three short paragraphs:

1. **Background.** One to two sentences on the clinical question or knowledge gap.
2. **Case presentation.** Four to six sentences: who the patient is (age, sex, relevant context), presenting complaint, key findings, working diagnosis, intervention, outcome.
3. **Conclusions.** One to two sentences on the lesson and its generalizability.

Worked abstract (illustrative; ~100 words):

```
A 10-year-old boy presented with a 4-year history of recurrent perioral rash unresponsive
to topical corticosteroids. A focused literature search using "dermatitis, perioral" and
"contact dermatitis, allergic" identified several reports describing perioral rashes from
toothpaste ingredients. Patch testing was positive for cinnamaldehyde, present in his
toothpaste. After he switched to a cinnamaldehyde-free toothpaste, the rash resolved
within two weeks and did not recur over 12 months of follow-up. Toothpaste allergy may
be more common than currently recognized because the diagnosis is easily missed; clinicians
should consider it in any patient with chronic perioral dermatitis.
```

### Case History / Report

This is drawn from chart notes but written for a reader who has never met the patient.

1. **Patient description.** Age, sex, ethnicity (when relevant), occupation, relevant medical background.
2. **History of presenting condition.** Onset, duration, character, severity, modifying factors.
3. **Physical examination.** Pertinent positive and pertinent negative findings only.
4. **Investigations.** Only the tests that informed the case. Include reference ranges for less commonly ordered tests. Do not list every routine result.
5. **Initial diagnosis and treatment.** State what was thought, what was tried, and why.
6. **Expected vs. actual outcome.** This is what makes the case worth reporting.
7. **Timeline.** Provide a CARE-style timeline figure or table of events from presentation to follow-up. Ready-to-use Mermaid templates (section-based and date-based) are in `references/diagrams.md`.

Avoid:

1. Irrelevant abnormal findings ("red herrings") unless they would mislead other clinicians.
2. Patient identifiers (full date of birth, exact admission dates if not necessary, hospital MR number).
3. Identifiable images without specific written consent for publication.

### Literature Search (the "Methods" of a Case Report)

This is what makes a modern case report a structured scientific observation rather than an anecdote. For PubMed-specific search basics (Boolean operators, MeSH, field tags, Clinical Queries), see `references/pubmed-essentials.md`.

1. **Databases.** Name them (MEDLINE/PubMed, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, regional databases such as LILACS or SciELO when relevant).
2. **Search terms.** Include MeSH headings and free-text terms; provide the exact strategy in a supplementary file.
3. **Date of last search.** Required.
4. **Results.** Number of relevant studies, brief synthesis of what is known.

Sentence skeleton:

1. `We searched MEDLINE (via PubMed), Embase, and the Cochrane CENTRAL register from inception to [DATE] using the MeSH terms [TERMS] combined with the free-text terms [TERMS]. The full search strategy is provided in Supplementary Appendix S1.`
2. `The search retrieved [N] reports, of which [N] were directly relevant. These reports describe [SYNTHESIS].`

Do not write a full systematic review inside the case report; the goal is to establish uniqueness and context.

### Discussion (the most important section)

Anchor the discussion to one question: **why is this case worth publishing?** Choose the elements most relevant to the case; do not include all subsections.

1. **Relevant literature in context.** Contrast the present case with the prior reports identified in the literature search. Be specific about what is new.
2. **Hypothesis or proposed mechanism.** A plausible explanation linking the new observation to existing knowledge.
3. **Diagnostic process.** Use a table or short algorithm to communicate the chronology and the differential narrowing. Graphics can replace 200 words.
4. **Outcomes.** Drug-drug interactions, drug-condition interactions, unexpected response, harm.
5. **Causality probability for drug-related cases.** For suspected adverse drug reactions, apply a validated probability scale and report the score. The most common is the **Naranjo Adverse Drug Reaction Probability Scale** (ten items, each scored +1, 0, or -1; total interpreted as definite ≥ 9, probable 5–8, possible 1–4, doubtful ≤ 0). Other tools include the WHO-UMC causality assessment and the Liverpool ADR Causality Assessment Tool. State the tool, the score, and the resulting category.
6. **Strengths and limitations.** Single observation cannot establish causality; selection of the case is non-random; outcome may be influenced by clinical context not fully reported.

Avoid framing the article as "Case report and review of the literature." Reviewers and editors discourage this hybrid form because the literature review and the case report are different article types serving different needs. Keep the literature integration brief and tied to the present observation.

Do not transfer an unread reference cited in another article. Read every cited paper yourself; misrepresented citations are an avoidable embarrassment.

### Medication-Specific Case Reports — Additional Detail

For case reports involving drugs, drug interactions, adverse drug reactions, medication errors, or pharmacokinetic events, capture the medication history with care. Required items:

1. **Medication list at presentation and during the case.** Each entry: generic name (and brand when relevant — different formulations can have different bioavailability, fillers, preservatives, dyes), strength, dosage form, route, schedule, start date, stop date.
2. **Include herbals, vaccines, depot injections, and over-the-counter medications.** State explicitly that the patient was asked about these classes.
3. **Discontinuation dates matter.** Some drugs (e.g., amiodarone, fluoxetine, biologics) have lasting effects for weeks or months after discontinuation.
4. **Adherence.** Verify and report the patient's medication adherence (self-report, pill count, electronic monitoring, pharmacy refill records).
5. **Renal function.** Serum creatinine, BUN, the calculation method used to estimate eGFR (CKD-EPI 2021, MDRD), urine output if catheterized.
6. **Hepatic function.** AST, ALT, total bilirubin, INR, serum albumin, A/G ratio.
7. **Allergy history.** Date of reaction, drug name (generic and brand), and reaction type. Be precise about combination products: a labeled "penicillin allergy" to ampicillin–sulbactam (Unasyn) or piperacillin–tazobactam (Zosyn) may actually be a sulbactam or tazobactam reaction; a "sulfa allergy" to trimethoprim–sulfamethoxazole (Septra, Bactrim) may be to trimethoprim rather than to the sulfonamide. Distinguish IgE-mediated allergy from other adverse reactions (e.g., drug-induced seizures).
8. **Drug serum levels.** Time drawn relative to the dose (peak, trough), total vs. free levels where applicable (phenytoin, valproic acid), assay method, and the laboratory's reference range (intra-laboratory variation is common).
9. **Diet history.** Food–drug interactions can change bioavailability or pharmacologic effect (grapefruit juice and CYP3A substrates; vitamin K-rich foods and warfarin; tyramine and MAOIs). Diet may also be a cause of "allergic" reactions misattributed to drugs.

### Causality Frameworks for Drug-Related Case Reports

| Framework | When to use | Score range |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Naranjo ADR Probability Scale | Single ADR, most common in ADR case reports | -4 to +13; definite ≥ 9 |
| WHO-UMC | Pharmacovigilance reporting | Categorical: certain / probable / possible / unlikely / unclassified |
| Liverpool ADR Causality Assessment | Paediatric and complex cases | Categorical |
| RUCAM | Drug-induced liver injury | -7 to +14 |
| Drug Interaction Probability Scale (DIPS) | Suspected drug-drug interactions | Definite / probable / possible / doubtful |

### Conclusions / Recommendations (the take-home message)

One or two short paragraphs answering:

1. What did this case teach the treating physician?
2. What recommendation can the authors offer to other clinicians?
3. What research question does this case open?

Sentence skeleton:

1. `In a patient with [PRESENTATION], [UNUSUAL FEATURE] led to [DIAGNOSIS / OUTCOME].`
2. `Clinicians should consider [DIAGNOSIS] when [TRIGGER FEATURE].`
3. `Whether [HYPOTHESIS] holds across a broader population warrants [PROSPECTIVE STUDY / SYSTEMATIC REVIEW].`

## Patient Perspective and Consent

Many journals (BMJ Case Reports, JMCR, BMC) require a brief Patient Perspective paragraph in which the patient describes the experience in their own voice. CARE recommends it. Even when not required, it is good practice.

Informed consent for publication is mandatory. The patient (or legal guardian, or next of kin if the patient is deceased) must give written consent. The journal may request the consent form; keep it on file. If consent is not possible (patient untraceable, deceased without next of kin), some journals require IRB or ethics committee approval to waive consent.

## Anonymization

1. Do not include name, full date of birth, exact admission/discharge dates if not essential, MR number, full address.
2. For images, mask identifying features (eyes, distinctive tattoos, jewelry) when possible; if not possible, obtain explicit consent for the image and state that consent in the manuscript.
3. Do not say "the only patient in our small hospital with this condition" if that uniqueness identifies the person.
4. Family details (rare consanguinity, named genetic syndrome in a small region) may be re-identifying; rephrase.

## Submission Logistics

1. **Word limit.** Most journals: 1,000 to 1,500 words for the body, with 10 to 20 references and 1 to 3 figures or tables.
2. **Author instructions.** Read them before drafting; format varies by journal.
3. **Uniform Requirements (ICMJE).** When the journal's instructions are silent, default to ICMJE.
4. **Where to submit.** General medical journals (BMJ Case Reports, JMCR, BMC), specialty journals, *NEJM* "Images in Clinical Medicine" or letters, *Lancet* "Case Reports", regional and society journals. Match the audience to the case.

## Common Reasons Case Reports Are Rejected

1. The case is not actually unique; a focused literature search would have found prior reports.
2. The "case report and literature review" hybrid pattern.
3. Patient identifiers or unconsented images.
4. Bloat: every test result, every paragraph of the chart, and a long historical literature review.
5. Conclusions overreach (a single case cannot establish causality).
6. Missing CARE items: timeline, patient perspective, consent statement.
7. Em-dashes and inconsistent terminology in the body text (see `references/manuscript-conventions.md`).
8. Citations not in order of first appearance, or fabricated references.

## Quick Quality Checklist

1. Is the case genuinely unique against a focused, dated literature search?
2. Is the structure CARE-aligned (title, abstract, patient information, clinical findings, timeline, diagnostic assessment, therapeutic intervention, follow-up and outcomes, discussion, patient perspective, informed consent)?
3. Is the timeline figure or table present?
4. Is the discussion focused on novelty and lesson, not on a parallel literature review?
5. Is the patient identifiable in any sentence, image, or supplementary file?
6. Is the conclusion specific and not overreaching?
7. Are tables and figures cited in order of first appearance?
8. Are all references real, verifiable, and in citation order?
9. Is the body text free of em-dashes (see `references/manuscript-conventions.md`)?
10. Is the manuscript prepared as a Word `.docx` file per journal style?
