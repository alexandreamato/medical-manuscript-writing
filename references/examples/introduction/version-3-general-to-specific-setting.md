# Introduction Version 3: General Problem, Then Specific Setting

`Version 3: Use when the broad problem is well known but the present study addresses a specific population, setting, or comparison (recommended when the setting is comparatively novel).`

## Skeleton

```
% Open with the general burden
[Condition] is associated with [burden] across populations.

% Narrow to the specific setting / population
This study focuses on [specific population] in [setting], where [special consideration]
makes the existing evidence base inadequate.
```

## Worked example (illustrative; fictional numbers)

```
Sepsis carries an in-hospital mortality of 25 to 30% globally and is the most common
cause of death in intensive care units. This study focuses on patients with sepsis
in low- and middle-income countries, where antimicrobial resistance, limited access to
microbiology, and constrained intensive-care capacity make extrapolation from high-income
trial populations problematic and where prospective evidence on early balanced crystalloid
resuscitation strategies is sparse.
```

## Notes

1. Move from the general (the global problem) to the specific (the population that the present study informs).
2. Make the special consideration concrete and methodological — why prior evidence does not generalize.
3. End the paragraph with a sentence that sets up the gap paragraph.
