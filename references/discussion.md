# Discussion and Conclusion Writing Guide (Medical Research)

## Goal

Close the paper with calibrated take-home messages, an honest account of strengths and limitations, and a clear statement of what should change in practice or research.

## Write the Discussion for the Reader, Not for Yourself

The purpose of the Discussion is to **explain the meaning of the results to the reader**. It is not a forum to demonstrate the depth of your knowledge of the field. The reader should finish the Discussion thinking, "That makes perfect sense — why hadn't I thought of that?". If the reader has to work through the Discussion twice to understand the message, the result will be overlooked, disregarded, and forgotten.

You should be thinking about the Discussion from the moment the study is conceived, not after the results table is finalized. Three questions to keep in mind throughout the project: Why is the study important? How does it relate to previous studies? What are the limitations?

## Length and Paragraph Discipline (Şanlı et al. 2013)

Practical limits that experienced authors and editors converge on:

1. **Total length:** the Discussion should not exceed the sum of Introduction + Methods + Results combined.
2. **Paragraph count:** 6 to 7 paragraphs total (introductory + intermediate + concluding); count and recount.
3. **Words per paragraph:** ≤ 200 words. One idea per paragraph.
4. **Sentence length:** ≤ 25 to 30 words. Previously mentioned information at the start; new information at the end.
5. **Voice:** active voice. Do not refrain from "We".
6. **Tense:** past for the present and prior study findings ("we found ..."); present for generalizations and conclusions ("this result suggests ...").

## "Divide and Conquer" for the Intermediate Paragraphs (Şanlı et al. 2013)

The middle of the Discussion is where readers lose interest. The strongest organizational tactic is **one paragraph per finding**, ordered by importance.

For each intermediate paragraph:

1. **Open with an indisputable topic sentence** — answer "What did we find?" plainly.
2. **State the result** in the context of one specific question or hypothesis.
3. **Compare with the literature**: present similar findings, then divergent findings, with the methodological reason for divergence.
4. **Address three diagnostic questions** in the same paragraph (Welch's prompts):
   - Could the discussed result be false or inadequate?
   - Why might it be false (inadequate blinding, protocol contamination, loss to follow-up, low statistical power)?
   - What does this outcome mean clinically?
5. **End with a concluding remark** that bridges to the next paragraph.

Avoid:

1. The need to mention every reference — you are not writing a review.
2. Repeating numerical values from the Results section unless essential.
3. Over-praising or over-criticizing other studies; both undermine credibility.
4. "Word salads" — long sentences with weak logical connection. Reviewers reject papers whose Discussion seems too difficult to repair.

## Persuasive-Argument Tactics (Makar et al. 2018)

Every interpretation in the Discussion should anticipate and absorb counterarguments rather than ignore them:

1. **Present alternative explanations** for each result, then justify why each alternative is unlikely. This builds the reader's confidence in your interpretation.
2. **Discuss non-significant (p > 0.05) and unanticipated results explicitly**, including their possible ramifications for the field. Hiding null findings is a common rejection trigger.
3. **Show how your findings resolve discrepancies among prior studies** — this is the single most effective way to convince readers that the study advances the field.
4. **Real-world relevance:** explicitly link the findings to clinical decisions, methodological progress, or economic concerns (cost, efficiency, quality of care).
5. **Use subheaders** when discussing multiple hypotheses or divergent concepts.
6. **Discuss results in the same order as in the Results section** so the reader can navigate.
7. **Be humble but not self-effacing.** A single study cannot answer every question; limitations are a springboard for future research, not a confession of failure.

## Discussion Structure

A well-structured Discussion has six paragraphs in this order. Adapt to journal length limits, but keep the order.

1. **Statement of principal findings.** One paragraph. Open with a direct, declarative, succinct sentence: `Our study showed [main finding].` Or: `In this [design], [intervention/exposure] [verb] [outcome]: [effect estimate, 95% CI].` Do not open with study-design verbiage or with "Table 1 summarizes ...". Match the wording of the Abstract conclusion.
2. **Comparison with prior evidence.** Place the result in the context of existing trials, cohorts, or systematic reviews. Discuss agreements and disagreements; explain disagreements with methodological reasons. Use phrases such as "Previous studies have shown ..." or "In contrast to our expectations ...".
3. **Possible mechanisms or alternative explanations.** Mechanism and biology come first, but **also** consider alternative explanations that contradict your bias. It is easy to consider only the explanations that fit the hypothesis. For observational studies, discuss residual confounding, reverse causation, and selection bias alongside the substantive interpretation. For RCTs, discuss the biological or clinical mechanism that could explain the observed effect.
4. **Strengths (optional).** Many editors prefer that you let the reader identify strengths rather than listing them yourself; explicit self-listing of strengths can read as self-congratulatory. If you do list strengths (preregistration, blinding, large sample size, validated outcome ascertainment, low loss to follow-up, sensitivity analyses), keep it short.
5. **Limitations.** State design and inference limitations honestly. If you do not, the reviewers and readers will. See the limitation guidance below.
6. **Implications.** State implications for clinical practice, policy, or future research. Distinguish: which patients does the result apply to, and which does it not apply to? Avoid overreach.

The Conclusion (often a separate short subsection or a final paragraph) is one to three sentences with the take-home message and the scope.

## Opening Sentence Examples

Use a direct factual opener. Examples (illustrative; not from real papers):

1. `Our findings suggest that [intervention/exposure] [reduced/did not reduce/was associated with] [outcome] in [population].`
2. `In this [physiological/clinical/randomized] study, [population] who received [intervention] showed [primary effect], suggesting [interpretation].`
3. `In contrast to previous reports, we observed [contrasting finding], suggesting [explanation].`

## Statistical vs. Clinical Significance

A statistically significant difference is not necessarily a clinically meaningful one, and a non-significant difference is not necessarily evidence of no effect.

1. State whether the magnitude of the observed effect crosses the threshold of clinical significance (the minimal clinically important difference).
2. For bench studies and large registries with low variance, point estimates can reach statistical significance with very small magnitudes; address this honestly.
3. For underpowered studies, a non-significant result with a wide CI does not establish equivalence; address this honestly too.

## Things to Avoid in the Discussion

These are the most common Discussion-section failures. Reviewers spot them on the first read.

1. **Brevity and verbosity.** Avoid one- and two-sentence paragraphs (collapse them into the preceding or following paragraph) and lengthy paragraphs that lose the reader. Aim for 100 to 200 words per paragraph, each focused on one idea.
2. **Repeating the Introduction.** Do not restate the burden of disease or the prior-evidence summary; the reader already read it.
3. **Repeating the Results.** Do not retabulate or rewrite the numbers; reference them and interpret them.
4. **Presenting new data.** All numbers in the Discussion must already appear in the Results. The Discussion interprets, it does not introduce.
5. **Overinterpretation.** "The data are the data: nothing more, nothing less." Do not extrapolate beyond what the design supports.
6. **Unwarranted speculation.** If you must speculate, mark it explicitly: `We speculate that ...`. Do not generalize beyond the studied population (e.g., from asthma to COPD) without flagging.
7. **Inflating importance.** Avoid "novel", "first study", "groundbreaking", "impressive". Let the data speak for themselves. A measure of humility goes a long way.
8. **Tangential issues.** Stay on the hypothesis and the results. Tangential issues dilute the message.
9. **The bully pulpit.** Do not criticize other studies disrespectfully, do not insult other investigators, and never preach to the reader.
10. **Conclusions not supported by the data.** Hypothesis, methods, data, and conclusions must form a tight package. If the data do not support the conclusion, change the conclusion, not the data.

## Statement of Principal Findings — Template

1. `In this [design] of [N] participants with [condition], [intervention/exposure] [verb] [outcome] compared with [comparator]: [effect estimate, 95% CI; p value if appropriate].`
2. `Secondary outcomes [were consistent / showed mixed effects], [briefly summarize].`
3. `[One sentence on safety / adverse events].`

Use causal language calibrated to the design. RCT: "reduced", "increased", "did not reduce". Observational: "was associated with", "was not associated with".

## Comparison with Prior Evidence — Template

1. `Our findings [are consistent with / extend / contradict] those of [study or systematic review].`
2. `[If consistent]: by [N times the size / in a different population / with longer follow-up], we strengthen the evidence base for ...`
3. `[If discordant]: the difference may be explained by [methodological reason: outcome definition, follow-up, population, era of treatment, statistical model].`

## Limitations — Guidance

Prefer limitations tied to the study scope, design, and inference:

1. **Design limitations** — observational design unable to establish causality; single-center recruitment; nonrandomized comparator; unblinded outcome assessor.
2. **Population / generalizability** — narrow eligibility, single ethnic background, single healthcare system, single era of treatment.
3. **Measurement limitations** — surrogate outcome, self-reported exposure, misclassification potential.
4. **Statistical limitations** — sample size, multiple testing, residual confounding (consider an E-value), missing data assumption.
5. **External validity** — translation to community practice, low-resource settings, or paediatric/elderly populations.

Avoid framing limitations only as "more research is needed" — be specific about what kind of study would strengthen which inference.

### Distinguish Limitation Types

1. **Threats to internal validity.** Do they change the direction of the effect, or the precision? State which.
2. **Threats to external validity.** Which patients, settings, or eras do the results not apply to?
3. **Reporting limitations.** Anything that you intended to do but could not (e.g., a planned subgroup analysis underpowered).

Do not bury fixable defects in the limitations paragraph; if a flaw is fixable, fix it before submission.

## Implications — Guidance

State two kinds of implications, separately:

1. **For practice or policy.** Only if the design supports it. RCT with definitive primary outcome → "These results support [practice change] in [population]." Observational study → "These results suggest that [hypothesis] warrants confirmation in a randomized trial."
2. **For future research.** Concrete: which question, which population, which design, which outcome.

## Conclusion — Template

1. `Among [population], [intervention/exposure] [verb] [outcome] compared with [comparator] over [duration].`
2. `[Optional: scope of generalizability].`
3. `[Optional: one sentence on next research step].`

Match the Abstract conclusion sentence-for-sentence. Numbers must agree.

## Quality Checklist

1. Does the first paragraph restate the primary finding in plain clinical language with the effect estimate and CI?
2. Is comparison with prior evidence specific (named studies or systematic reviews) and balanced?
3. Are mechanisms or explanations discussed without overreach?
4. Is each limitation specific, with its inferential consequence?
5. Are implications calibrated to what the design can support?
6. Does the Conclusion match the Abstract conclusion?
7. Are causal verbs ("caused", "reduced") only used where the design supports them?
8. Are tables, figures, and citations in the Discussion already cited earlier in the manuscript? (The Discussion should not introduce new figures or numbers that did not appear in Results.)
