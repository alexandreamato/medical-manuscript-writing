# Writing Process and Order (Medical Research)

This file complements the section guides. It covers the parts of the workflow that are not section-specific: when to write what, in what order, and what to verify before submission. The tips are drawn from Mateu Arrom et al. (Actas Urol Esp 2018) "How to write an original article", McCarthy & Reilly (Fam Med 2000) for case reports, and current ICMJE Uniform Requirements.

## 1. Before Writing — Four Self-Criticism Questions

Before drafting any paper, answer these four honestly:

1. Have I done new and interesting work?
2. Is the work related to a current topic in the field?
3. Is there solid evidence and a clear answer to the prespecified hypothesis or aim?
4. Are the conclusions of the study relevant to clinicians, researchers, or policy?

If the answer to any is "no", do not start writing. Revise the question, gather more data, or shelve the project. Writing cannot rescue a paper that has nothing new to say.

## 2. Pick the Right Article Type and Journal

The article type follows the data, not the other way around:

1. **Original article (IMRaD).** Confirmatory or descriptive empirical studies.
2. **Systematic review or meta-analysis.** PRISMA-aligned synthesis.
3. **Case report or case series.** Genuinely unique clinical observations (CARE-aligned). See `references/case-report.md`.
4. **Letter to the editor.** Brief response, observation, or short case.
5. **Editorial / commentary / perspective.** Opinion or framing of an issue.
6. **Brief report / research letter.** Short empirical contribution (often 800 to 1,500 words).
7. **Protocol.** Trial protocol (SPIRIT) or systematic-review protocol (PRISMA-P).
8. **Clinical image.** Single instructive image with a short caption.

Choose the journal before drafting:

1. List candidate journals from the references most relevant to your topic.
2. Check scope, indexing (PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science), peer-review system, time to first decision, and citation rate.
3. Do not chase impact factor alone — mismatched scope is a common rejection cause.
4. Read the journal's Instructions to Authors carefully. Format the manuscript to those rules from the start; reviewers do not want to be reformatted.

## 3. Recommended Drafting Order

Most researchers write in submission order (Introduction → Methods → Results → Discussion → Abstract). This is the slowest and most error-prone way. The following order produces fewer rewrites:

1. **Tables and figures first.** Build the primary outcome table, the baseline characteristics table (Table 1), and the participant flow diagram before writing prose. The shape of the data dictates the prose.
2. **Results.** Write the prose around the tables and figures, in past tense, without repeating the numbers in the tables.
3. **Methods.** Write Methods next, also in past tense. Include only information available at study design (anything obtained from the data goes to Results).
4. **Introduction.** Three paragraphs: what is the problem, what is unsolved, what does this paper answer.
5. **Discussion.** Anchor each paragraph to the structure in `references/discussion.md`.
6. **Abstract last.** Once the body is final, write the abstract; this is when you know exactly what to highlight.
7. **Title last.** Short, explanatory, and descriptive of the design (e.g., "A randomized trial of ...", "A prospective cohort study of ...").

## 3b. IMRaD Variations

Most original-research articles follow the standard IMRaD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion). Two common variations:

1. **Combined Results and Discussion.** Some journals interweave the two — each result is presented and immediately interpreted. Useful for complex multi-experiment studies where carrying findings from a separate Results section to a separate Discussion section becomes hard to follow. Verify whether the target journal allows this format before drafting.
2. **IaMRDC (Mondal et al. 2019).** Adds explicit `aim` after Introduction and a separate `Conclusion` after Discussion. Encourages authors to state the aim distinctly from the Introduction and the conclusion distinctly from the Discussion. Useful as an authoring discipline even when the journal expects standard IMRaD headings.
3. **ILMRaD.** Adds a separate **L**iterature Review section between Introduction and Methods. More common in theses and dissertations than in journal articles.

For systematic reviews, case reports, and other special article types, the structure differs further; see the type-specific files (`references/systematic-review.md`, `references/narrative-review.md`, `references/case-report.md`).

## 4. IMRaD Question-Mapping

Each IMRaD section answers one question. Keep it tight.

| Section | Question | Tense |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Introduction | Why was it done? | Mostly present (established knowledge), past for prior studies |
| Methods | How was it done? | Past |
| Results | What was found? | Past |
| Discussion | What is the relevance? | Mixed: past for own study, present for established knowledge |

If a paragraph answers a question that does not match its section, move it.

## 5. What Goes Where (Common Mistakes)

| Information | Belongs in | Common mistake |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Eligibility criteria | Methods | Sometimes drifts into Results |
| Number of participants enrolled | Results (and flow diagram) | Pre-stated in Methods, then duplicated |
| Baseline characteristics | Results (Table 1) | Sometimes placed in Methods |
| Statistical model description | Methods | Often appears for the first time inside Results |
| Comparison with prior studies | Discussion | Often placed in Introduction |
| Speculation about mechanism | Discussion | Often placed in Introduction or Results |
| Primary outcome point estimate | Results | Often previewed in Introduction |
| Conclusions / clinical implications | Discussion / Conclusion | Sometimes appear in Results |

Decision rule: if a sentence is needed before the data are seen, it belongs to Introduction or Methods. If a sentence depends on the data, it belongs to Results or Discussion.

## 6. Writing Quality — Practical Tips

1. **Read the manuscript aloud** at least once before submission. Non-fluent prose becomes audible.
2. **Have a non-specialist read it.** A colleague outside the topic should understand the paper after one read; if they cannot, the writing is too dense.
3. **Internal consistency check.** Every number in the abstract appears in Results; every analysis in Results is described in Methods; every claim in Discussion is supported by Results.
4. **Do not diminish prior authors' work.** Discuss limitations of prior studies methodologically, not rhetorically.
5. **Do not overstate.** A small effect with a wide CI is a small effect with a wide CI; do not call it "promising" without justification.
6. **English-language polish.** If submitting in English to a journal that expects native-quality prose, consult a fluent colleague or a professional editing service. Reviewers conflate poor English with poor science.
7. **Do not draw conclusions that the design cannot support.** If the study did not measure cost, do not discuss cost-effectiveness. If the design is observational, do not say "caused".
8. **Limit references to those that contribute.** Prefer original articles over reviews. Prefer recent references (often within 5 years) where appropriate; classics are fine when classic.
9. **No grammatical errors.** Run a spell-check and a grammar check; have a second reader specifically for grammar.
10. **No em-dashes in body sentences.** See `references/manuscript-conventions.md`.
11. **Cite each table and figure in the text in order of appearance.** Renumber after every revision.
12. **Take reviewer comments seriously.** Reviewers exist to improve the paper. Respond point-by-point with evidence; do not dismiss.

## 7. Two Mottos to Keep in Mind

1. **"The title sells the paper."** Spend disproportionate time on the title. Reviewers, search engines, and clinicians all triage by title. A descriptive, accurate, succinct title (often a `title: subtitle` form) outperforms a clever or vague one.
2. **"Make your point with data, not arguments."** Do not write "the intervention had a strong effect"; write "mortality was 12% vs. 18% (RR 0.67; 95% CI, 0.53 to 0.84)". Numbers are persuasive; adjectives are not.

## 8. Do Not Sit on Drafts — the "Ten Other Groups" Pressure

By the time you conceived the project, ten other groups were already working on it; five may have already published it. Iterate quickly:

1. Aim for a complete first draft within 4 to 6 weeks of finalizing the analysis.
2. Send drafts to co-authors with explicit deadlines (typically 1 to 2 weeks per round).
3. Do not perfect the manuscript indefinitely; submit, accept reviewer feedback, and improve from there.
4. Avoid redundant or duplicate publication. Salami-slicing into multiple small papers is discouraged; pack the manuscript with the data needed to answer the question and let it stand.

## 9. Pure Replications Are Harder to Publish

Confirmatory studies — replication of a known finding without methodological strengthening, a new population, or a new analytic contribution — face a higher publication bar. If your study is largely confirmatory, identify what is genuinely additive (larger sample, longer follow-up, different population, prespecified subgroup, new analytic method) and frame the contribution around that addition.

## 9b. Pre-Peer Review (Şanlı et al. 2013)

Before submission, get a "pre-peer review" from internal and external readers. This is one of the highest-leverage steps in the whole process.

1. **General internal reviewer.** A colleague from a different specialty who reads for simplicity, clarity, and effectiveness. Will spot dense passages and unexplained jargon.
2. **Expert internal reviewer.** A senior colleague familiar with the topic. Will provide subject-matter feedback and catch overstated claims.
3. **External reviewer.** A colleague who did not contribute to the data and has no stake in the manuscript. Most objective.

Before sending the manuscript, contact each reviewer to confirm they have time and to brief them on the topic. Ask them to answer four questions explicitly:

1. Does the manuscript contribute to the literature?
2. Is it persuasive?
3. Is it suitable for the selected journal?
4. Is the language simple, clear, and effective?

Use the "incubation" period (waiting for feedback) to recheck the literature for new publications.

## 9c. Three-Pass Self-Review

After completing the manuscript, review the whole thing **three times**, separated by time.

1. **First pass — logic and organization.** Does each section answer its assigned question? Does the argument hold from Introduction through Conclusion?
2. **Second pass — syntax, grammar, terminology consistency.**
3. **Third pass — after 1 to 2 weeks of "cooling".** Reading the manuscript with fresh eyes catches errors and unclear passages that were invisible the day after writing.

For a deeper structured self-reading from three reader perspectives (skeptical methodologist, busy clinician, non-specialist), see `references/read-as-reader.md`. That technique sits between the three-pass review here and the formal pre-submission checklist in `references/paper-review.md`.

## 9d. Daily-Writing vs. Burst Strategies (Şanlı et al. 2013)

Two viable approaches:

| Strategy | Time per session | Total time to complete | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Daily | 30 minutes/day, 5 days/week | Several weeks | Maintains motivation; habit-forming; less re-orientation cost | Slow; risk of obsolescence in fast-moving fields |
| Burst | 4 to 6 hours/day for ~1 week | One week | Rapid completion; deep focus | Boring after several days; daily clinical workload disruption |

The daily strategy is generally preferred unless a deadline forces a burst. **Begin writing before the study is finalized**: drafting Introduction and Methods during data collection helps catch missing analyses while they are still fixable.

## 9e. The Most Common Manuscript-Level Mistake — Loss of Focus

Şanlı et al. identify "loss of the focus of the study" as the dominant manuscript-level problem. Symptoms:

1. Multiple main ideas in the same text.
2. Unrelated results presented to "reinforce" the main claim.
3. Tangential issues that dilute the message.

Diagnostic question to ask at every stage of writing: **"What is the objective of the study?"** If you cannot answer in one sentence, the manuscript has drifted.

## 10. Word, Reference, and Display-Item Budgets (Typical Targets)

These are typical journal targets; verify against the Instructions to Authors.

| Element | Original article | Brief report / letter | Case report | Systematic review |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Body word count | 2,500 to 4,000 | 800 to 1,500 | 1,000 to 1,500 | 4,000 to 6,000 |
| Abstract | 250 to 350 (structured) | 150 to 200 | 150 to 200 | 250 to 350 (structured) |
| References | 30 to 50 | 10 to 20 | 10 to 20 | 50 to 100+ |
| Tables and figures | 4 to 7 total | 1 to 3 | 1 to 3 | 3 to 6 plus PRISMA flow |

Aim for the lower end. Reviewers reward concision.

## 11. Three-Layer Pre-Submission Pass

Run all three before clicking submit.

### Layer 1 — Content

1. Are all four self-criticism questions answered "yes"?
2. Does each IMRaD section answer the right question?
3. Are limitations stated honestly?
4. Are conclusions calibrated to the design?

### Layer 2 — Structure and reporting

1. Is the relevant reporting checklist (CONSORT/STROBE/PRISMA/STARD/CARE/TRIPOD) complete?
2. Is the participant flow diagram present?
3. Is each table and figure cited in the text in order of appearance?
4. Are the numbers in the abstract identical to the numbers in the Results?

### Layer 3 — Mechanics

1. Are all references real, verifiable, and in citation order?
2. Are author affiliations, ORCIDs, and corresponding-author details correct?
3. Is the body free of em-dashes?
4. Is the file in `.docx` per most medical journals' requirements?
5. Is the cover letter prepared (one page: brief summary, why the paper fits the journal, statement of original work and no concurrent submission)?

## 12. After Acceptance

1. Proofread the typeset version for typesetting errors; do not introduce new content.
2. Verify your name and ORCID, your affiliation, and the corresponding-author details.
3. Verify that figures retain resolution and that table alignment did not break in typesetting.
4. Sign off only after the proof matches the accepted manuscript.

## 13. After Publication — Track and Respond

1. Track citations and post-publication discussion (PubPeer, Letters to the Editor).
2. Respond to substantive Letters to the Editor with a calibrated reply.
3. If a substantive error is identified, follow ICMJE guidance: errata for typos, corrigenda for substantive corrections, retraction for unfixable errors.
